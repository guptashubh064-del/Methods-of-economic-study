Methods of
Economic Study

Two big ways economists figure out how the world works — explained simply, like you're 10.

🧠
Deductive Method
Also called: Analytical Method / Abstract Method / A Priori Method

You start with a general rule that you already know or assume is true — then you apply it to specific cases to draw conclusions.

Think of it like: "I already have the answer in my head. Let me see if the real world matches it."

🔬
Inductive Method
Also called: Historical Method / Empirical Method / A Posteriori Method

You start by observing many real-life examples and collecting facts — then you build a general rule from those observations.

Think of it like: "I don't know the answer yet. Let me look at the world first and figure it out."

🎯 Simple Analogy — Detective Style

🧠 Deductive

Sherlock Holmes says: "All criminals leave footprints. There are footprints here. Therefore, a criminal was here." He started with a rule and applied it.

🔬 Inductive

A detective visits 100 crime scenes, notices footprints in 95 of them, and then concludes: "Criminals usually leave footprints." He built the rule from observations.

History of Both Methods
Ancient Greece — 300s BC

Roots of Deductive Thinking — Aristotle

The great Greek philosopher Aristotle developed the concept of deductive reasoning. He believed you could reach truth by starting from known principles and reasoning logically — without always needing experiments. This became the foundation for the deductive method.

1700s — The Classical Economists

Adam Smith, Ricardo, and the Deductive Era

Economists like Adam Smith (Wealth of Nations, 1776) and David Ricardo used deductive reasoning heavily. They created theories about supply, demand, wages, and rent by thinking logically from assumed human behaviour — without tons of data collection. Ricardo especially built abstract economic models from general assumptions.

1600s — Francis Bacon

Father of the Inductive Method

Francis Bacon, the English philosopher, challenged Aristotle's pure reasoning. He said: "Don't just think — observe!" He promoted gathering real-world data and building conclusions from it. This was a revolution in scientific thinking and became the backbone of the inductive method.

1800s — The Historical / German School

Roscher, Hildebrand & the Inductive Economists

German economists like Wilhelm Roscher and Bruno Hildebrand strongly opposed abstract theories. They believed economics must be based on historical facts and real data. This "Historical School" pushed the inductive method into mainstream economic thought.

Late 1800s — Alfred Marshall

The Great Synthesis

Alfred Marshall, one of the greatest economists, argued that neither method alone is enough. He said both deductive and inductive methods should be used together — like two blades of a scissors. Modern economics largely follows this combined approach.

Steps / Process in Each Method

🟠 Deductive Method — Steps

1

Lay Down Assumptions — The economist starts with basic assumptions about human behaviour. E.g., "People always try to earn maximum profit."

2

Build a Hypothesis — Based on assumptions, a hypothesis (a testable idea) is created. E.g., "If wages rise, workers will work more hours."

3

Apply Logic / Reasoning — Using mathematics or pure logic, the hypothesis is developed into a theory without actually going out to collect data.

4

Verify with Real Data — Finally, the conclusion is tested against real-world facts to check if the theory holds true.

5

Establish Economic Laws — If verified, the hypothesis becomes an economic law or principle that can be applied broadly.

🔵 Inductive Method — Steps

1

Observation of Facts — The economist first observes and collects facts from the real world through surveys, experiments, or historical records.

2

Classification of Data — The collected facts are sorted, arranged, and classified into meaningful categories for easy analysis.

3

Analysis and Generalization — Patterns are spotted in the data and a general conclusion is drawn. E.g., "In every observed case, prices rose when supply fell."

4

Formulation of a Law — The generalization becomes an economic law. E.g., "Law of Supply: As price rises, supply also rises."

5

Verification — The law is tested again with new observations to confirm it works across different cases and time periods.

Merits & Demerits

✅ Merits of Deductive

  • 💡 Simple and easy — doesn't require tons of data collection. Pure logic is enough.
  • ⚡ Quick to use — forming theories is faster when you start from general principles.
  • 🔭 Helpful in complex topics — where collecting real data is nearly impossible.
  • 📐 Precise and systematic — because it uses mathematical logic and reasoning.
  • 🌍 Universal laws — conclusions often apply broadly across different economies.

❌ Demerits of Deductive

  • 🚫 Based on assumptions — if assumptions are wrong, the whole theory collapses.
  • 🏔️ Ignores real-world complexity — human behaviour is not always logical or predictable.
  • 📊 No statistical proof — conclusions are not backed by actual data or statistics.
  • 🔒 Narrow scope — abstract theories may not apply to all real situations.
  • ⚠️ Risk of error — incorrect premises lead to incorrect economic laws.

✅ Merits of Inductive

  • 🌱 Based on real facts — conclusions come from actual observations, not guesses.
  • 📈 More realistic — it accounts for actual human behaviour and real-world conditions.
  • 🔍 Statistical support — uses data to back up claims, making them verifiable.
  • 🧪 Scientific approach — similar to how natural sciences work, making it credible.
  • 🔄 Dynamic — can be updated as new facts come in, making laws flexible.

❌ Demerits of Inductive

  • ⏳ Very time-consuming — collecting and analysing large amounts of data takes huge effort.
  • 💸 Expensive — surveys, fieldwork, and data analysis require money and resources.
  • 🎭 Risk of bias — the researcher's personal views may affect how data is collected or interpreted.
  • 🔢 Requires experts — statistical analysis needs trained professionals; not everyone can do it.
  • 📉 Limited generalization — conclusions from one place/time may not apply everywhere.

🏆 Which is Better?

Neither method alone is perfect. Alfred Marshall said it best: both methods are like the two blades of a scissors — you need both to cut properly. Modern economists use a combined approach — they build theories using deduction and then verify them using inductive, real-world data. This is called the Hypothetico-Deductive Method in modern economics.
